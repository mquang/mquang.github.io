Before answering you why I left the U.S I wanted to tell you why I decided to go to the U.S.

           When I was in Vietnam I was just a typical Asian kid trying to study hard getting all A so my parents wouldn't be ashamed of me and really I was just trying to meet others expectations and I wasn't living my own life, I didn't know who I was and I didn't know what I was doing until 11th grade. That was the first time I got exposed to the American culture by an American English teacher and he was so passionate talking about American dream, the freedom of speech, the higher educational system and I was so fascinated about his stories.

As an international student I was not allowed to work more than 20 hours during the school year and I couldn't work outside of the campus if the job didn't correlate with my major. So that's the reason why I double major in marketing and finance so that I could get an internship in marketing. I had to fill out so many paperworks, sent my résumé to hundreds of companies and got rejected because I'm an international student, whereas in Vietnam is less competitive and there are actually more job opportunities for people with higher skills both foreigners and Vietnamese citizens.

I saw skyscrapers and people using higher technologies, and Vietnamese people are so young, very energetic, very smart, and especially they are very studious, and I also realize that it's easier to become an entrepreneur in Vietnam. For example if you have a bicycle and you know how to make sandwiches you can open your own mobile Subway business and you don't have to worry about getting sued if your sandwich is not one foot long. Besides that I feel like Vietnam as a developing country will have more room for me to shine and there will be more people for me to help and inspire. I feel like I can contribute more in Vietnam than in the U.S working a 9 to 5 job.

        OK so you might argue with me that America provides opportunities for everyone who pursues their dream and works hard for what they aim for right. I agree and I did that. I worked my ass off and finally got a job as a marketing manager but I wasn't feeling happy. The work-life integration in America is too stressful and competitive. I remember when I first came to America I was so shocked seeing American people work so hard and rush through life. They eat fast, they have to go coffee everywhere, they walk fast like somebody is chasing them and they just overwork themselves. Actually there's a study show that half of the American people don't take vocations because they either feel guilty or they are afraid of being replaced. And I was in that situation as well. I remember having meetings after meetings, trying to work extra hours, coming home all exhausted and repeating. I was trapped in a rat race and didn't know how to escape.

           Then COVID hit, I took a step back and really asked myself what I wanted to do in life so I decided to make a leap and move back to Vietnam and start my new life here and it was the best decision of my life. The life here is so chill and so relaxing. People wake up early and you can see they enjoy their coffee and talk to their colleagues before going to work. Not like me in America, I was eating lunch and writing proposals at my desk. People here have lunch with their friends and they even have one and a half hour nap. Vietnamese people work hard as well especially in the big cities like Hanoi or Saigon you know. You can see the hustle and bustle in the big cities but people know how to enjoy their life. They love "Nhậu" meaning drinking, talking, eating with their friends every day.
