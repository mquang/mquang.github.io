under the weather /ˈweðər/ (idiom) không được khỏe ( ill)
  • I'm feeling a bit under the weather - I think I'm getting a cold.
  • I hear you've been a bit under the weather. Are you feeling better now?
  • Mike's feeling a little under the weather so he couldn't come tonight.

    hold up (ph.v)
    1. duy trì (to remain strong or stay in a fairly good condition, esp. after being repaired or being ill)

    2. cản trở, làm chậm trễ (to delay someone or something)
    		 1.
  • His physical condition has held up well.

    • 2.
  • Sorry I’m late – I was held up at work.
  • An accident is holding up traffic.
    		•
    give out (ph.v) hỏng, kiệt sức, cạn kiệt năng lượng (if a machine or part of your body gives out, it stops working)
  • The batteries are likely to give out soon.
  • At the end of the race his legs gave out and he collapsed on the ground.
    		•
    wear somebody out (ph.v) làm ai đó mệt mỏi (to make someone extremely tired)
    wear - wore - worn
  • Walking around a museum all day really wears me out.
  • The kids have totally worn me out.
    		•
    put aside /əˈsaɪd/ (ph.v)
    1. tiết kiệm (to save an amount of money so that you can buy or pay for something in the future)

    2. dành (thời gian) (to keep a period of time free so that you can use it for a particular purpose)
    		 1.
  • Each month she puts some money aside for her retirement.

    • 2.
  • He tries to put some time aside every evening to read to the kids.
  • I put aside half an hour every day to write my diary.
    		•
    fall behind (ph.v)
    1. chậm (to fail to do something or pay something at the time that you should)

    2. tụt hậu, tụt lại phía sau, không theo kịp mọi người
    		 1.
  • Severe weather conditions caused the construction project to fall behind by over three months.
  • If we fall behind schedule we may lose the contract.
  • We started to fall behind with our mortgage payments when my husband lost his job.

    • 2.
  • He was ill for six weeks and fell behind with his schoolwork.
  • After five miles Tara was tired and started to fall behind.
    		•
    take on (ph.v) nhận (thêm) trách nhiệm, công việc (to agree to do some work or to be responsible for something; to accept additional responsibility)
  • I can’t take on any more work at the moment.
  • I wanted to make a good impression, so I took on a lot of tasks at work.
  • We don't have enough time to cover all of this in class, so you'll need to take it on yourselves if you want to learn more.
    		•
    split up (ph.v) chia tay, li dị (to end a marriage or relationship)
  • She split up with her boyfriend last week.
  • Her parents split up a few months ago.
    		•
    revolve around sb/sth /rɪˈvɑːlv/ (ph.v) xoay quanh
  • to have somebody or something as the main interest or subject
  • to move around something in a circle

    		•  1.
  • Jane’s life revolves around her children.
  • The discussion revolved around the question of changing the club's name.
  • His whole life revolves around football.
    • think the world revolves around you: to think that other people and things are not as important as you are
  • She seems to think that the world revolves around her (=that she is the only important person).

    • 2.
  • The Moon revolves around the Earth.
    		•
